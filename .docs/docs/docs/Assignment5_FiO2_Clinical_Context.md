# Assignment 5 – Fraction of Inspired Oxygen (FiO₂) and Its Importance in Emergency Triage

Fraction of Inspired Oxygen (FiO₂) refers to the percentage of oxygen that a person breathes in. It is an important measurement used in healthcare because it indicates how much oxygen is being delivered to the lungs. Under normal conditions, the air we breathe contains approximately **21% oxygen**, meaning that the FiO₂ of room air is 21%. When a patient is experiencing breathing difficulties or low oxygen levels, healthcare professionals can provide supplemental oxygen to increase the FiO₂ and help the body receive the oxygen it needs.

The normal FiO₂ for a healthy person breathing room air is **21% (0.21)**. In a hospital setting, FiO₂ can be increased depending on the patient's condition and oxygen requirements, reaching up to **100% (1.0)** in critically ill patients. Higher FiO₂ levels are commonly used for patients suffering from respiratory illnesses, severe infections, trauma, or other conditions that affect the body's ability to absorb oxygen effectively.

In emergency triage, FiO₂ helps healthcare professionals determine how severe a patient's respiratory condition may be. Patients who require higher levels of oxygen support are often more seriously ill and may be prioritized for urgent assessment and treatment. For example, conditions such as pneumonia, asthma attacks, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) can significantly increase a patient's oxygen requirements. Monitoring FiO₂ allows clinicians to identify patients who may be at risk of respiratory failure and prioritize them accordingly.

One of the main advantages of FiO₂ is that it provides a clear indication of how much respiratory support a patient needs. If a patient's condition improves, the amount of supplemental oxygen required will often decrease. On the other hand, increasing oxygen requirements may indicate that the patient's condition is worsening. This makes FiO₂ a useful tool for monitoring patient progress and evaluating the effectiveness of treatment.

However, FiO₂ should not be considered on its own. Healthcare professionals evaluate it alongside other vital signs and clinical measurements such as oxygen saturation (SpO₂), respiratory rate, heart rate, and blood pressure. For example, a patient who requires a high FiO₂ but still has low oxygen saturation may be experiencing severe respiratory distress and require more advanced interventions. This highlights the importance of FiO₂ in emergency triage, as it helps clinicians identify critically ill patients, prioritize treatment, and make timely decisions that can improve patient outcomes.
