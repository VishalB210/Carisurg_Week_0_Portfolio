# Assignment 4 – Pulse Rate and Its Importance in Emergency Triage

Pulse rate, also known as heart rate, is the number of times a person's heart beats per minute (bpm). It is one of the key vital signs measured when a patient arrives at the emergency department because it provides information about how the cardiovascular system is functioning and whether the body is under stress. Pulse can be measured manually at locations such as the wrist or neck, or automatically using medical monitoring equipment.

For most adults, a normal resting pulse rate ranges between **60 and 100 bpm**. A pulse rate below 60 bpm is referred to as **bradycardia**, while a pulse rate above 100 bpm is known as **tachycardia**. These values can vary depending on factors such as age, physical fitness, medication use, and underlying medical conditions.

In emergency triage, pulse rate helps healthcare professionals quickly identify patients who may require urgent attention. An elevated pulse rate may be associated with conditions such as pain, fever, dehydration, blood loss, infection, or shock. Conversely, an unusually low pulse rate may indicate heart conduction problems, medication effects, or other serious medical concerns. Because changes in pulse rate can be an early indicator of clinical deterioration, it is routinely assessed alongside other vital signs such as blood pressure, respiratory rate, temperature, and oxygen saturation. Patients presenting with extremely high or low pulse rates may be assigned a higher triage priority because these abnormalities can indicate potentially life-threatening conditions that require rapid assessment and intervention.

One reason pulse rate is so useful in triage is that it can be measured quickly, easily, and non-invasively. By combining pulse measurements with other clinical observations, healthcare providers can make faster decisions about patient priority, monitoring requirements, and treatment needs. Overall, pulse rate is a simple but powerful tool that helps clinicians assess patient condition and support effective emergency care.
